# Candidate: Casey Neistat

## Why This Story
Casey is arguably the purest embodiment of the "creator replaces the studio" narrative. He went from making indie films with no budget to becoming one of the most influential video creators in the world — entirely outside the traditional studio system.

## The Arc

### Background
- Casey grew up without much, dropped out of high school, moved to NYC.
- In the early 2000s, he and his brother Van made a short film "iPod's Dirty Secret" (2003) criticizing Apple's battery replacement policy. It went viral in the pre-YouTube era — one of the earliest examples of an individual using video to take on a corporation and win. Apple changed their policy.
- He made an HBO series (The Neistat Brothers, ~2010) — a traditional media deal. It was critically appreciated but didn't lead to a traditional media career.

### The YouTube Pivot
- Starting around 2015, Casey began daily vlogging on YouTube. No production team, no studio — just him, a camera, and NYC.
- His filmmaking quality was genuinely cinematic — drone shots, time lapses, creative editing — but produced by essentially one person.
- He demonstrated that a single creator with taste and skill could produce content that *looked and felt* as good as studio productions.
- His channel grew to millions of subscribers. He became the template for the "one-person media company."

### Later Arc
- Founded Beme (a social media company), sold it to CNN for ~$25M. [VERIFY]
- Continued creating but shifted formats over time — less daily vlogging, more project-based content.
- His influence spawned an entire generation of vloggers and creator-filmmakers.

## The Parallel to Software
- Casey is the prototype of the "content creator" who replaced the studio. One person, right tools, genuine taste.
- The tools (DSLR cameras, drones, editing software) didn't make everyone Casey Neistat — they made it *possible* for someone like Casey to exist without a studio behind him.
- Similarly: coding agents won't make everyone a great software creator, but they remove the institutional bottleneck that prevented individuals with great product taste from building things.

## Strengths as a Cold Open
- Very visual, concrete story. Easy to narrate.
- The "iPod's Dirty Secret" anecdote is a perfect Gladwell-style opening scene.
- Clean arc: outsider with taste beats institutions with resources.

## Weaknesses
- Maybe *too* on-the-nose? The creator economy parallel is so direct it might feel obvious.
- Casey's story has been told a lot. Less novelty for readers who follow tech/media.
