# The Software Content Creator: Outline

## Description candidates
- "Software is about to have its YouTube moment."
- "Nobody watches movies in theaters anymore, and everyone watches more video than ever."
- "When code stops being a secret, taste becomes the product."

---

## Arc 1: The Story (Conan + Bon Iver)

**P1: The cabin.**
Open with the image of Justin Vernon in a Wisconsin cabin, winter 2006. Heartbroken, sick, alone. A $100 microphone, a laptop, and a 1964 Silvertone guitar he got fixed by trading venison to a local luthier.

**P2: What came out of the cabin.**
For Emma, Forever Ago. 500 self-pressed CDs. Discovered by a blog called "My Old Kentucky Blog." Within a year, signed to a label. Within five, a Grammy for Best New Artist, beating Nicki Minaj.

**P3: The setup for the larger point.**
Vernon didn't beat the recording studio by being a better studio. He made the studio irrelevant. The constraints of working alone, with basic tools, became the album's defining quality. What he had that no studio could give him was taste.

**P4: Transition to the broader pattern.**
Vernon's story is one instance of something that's happened over and over in the last two decades. When the tools to create something become cheap and widely available, the thing that differentiates creators is no longer access to the tools. It's what they do with them.

**P5: Conan, the other side of the same coin.**
Conan O'Brien spent 28 years in late night television. He inherited Late Night from Letterman in 1993, survived a brutal NBC controversy in 2010, and landed at TBS. He was one of the best to ever do the format.

**P6: The format stopped working.**
Late night viewership has dropped up to 80% over the last decade. Fallon's Tonight Show pulls 1.2 million viewers where Leno pulled 5 million. The shows converged on the same playbook: political monologues optimized for next-day YouTube clips. The whimsy and diversity of the old format collapsed.

**P7: Conan left.**
In 2021, he walked away from TBS. Not because he failed, but because the format had become a constraint. He started a podcast, "Conan O'Brien Needs a Friend." It's hit 427 million downloads. Team Coco sold to SiriusXM for $150 million. He launched a travel show on Max with a 94% on Rotten Tomatoes.

**P8: The punchline (Conan's own words).**
Conan himself put it well: "This old format is going away, but they're being replaced by a multitude of other ways to connect with people and be funny... that in some ways are more freeing." In 2025, Letterman presented him the Mark Twain Prize. The institution honored him after he'd already outgrown it.

**P9: The irony that sets up Arc 2.**
Here's the thing that isn't immediately obvious. People didn't stop watching video. They watch more video than ever. Theaters are declining, but total hours of video consumed is at an all time high. The demand exploded. It just fragmented.

## Arc 2: The Pivot to Software

**P10: So what does this have to do with software?**
I've spent the last few years building benchmarks for AI coding agents. SWE-bench, which I created in [year], measures how well AI agents can resolve real GitHub issues. Two years ago, it was hard to imagine models doing well on it. Today, they're at 90%.

**P11: What that trajectory means concretely.**
At some point on this curve, the cost of building a full-stack software product drops to near zero. Not the cost of designing it, or understanding who it's for, or earning trust. Just the implementation. The code itself.

**P12: Software stops being a secret.**
Today, technology companies are valuable in part because the technology is closed source and hard to replicate. Building a competitor to [X product] isn't just a matter of wanting to. You'd need a large engineering team and months of work. That barrier is collapsing.

**P13: Introduce RevEng as evidence.**
I've been working on a project called RevEng where we give AI agents a black-box executable. The agent can observe the system's behavior but has no access to source code. The task: reconstruct the software from scratch. [Brief description of results / what's possible.] This isn't hypothetical.

**P14: What this doesn't disrupt (honesty beat).**
To be clear, not all software becomes trivially reproducible. Products with strong network effects, deep data moats, or regulatory constraints don't suddenly lose their position because code is cheap to write. Airbnb's value is its two-sided marketplace, not its Rails app.

**P15: What this does disrupt.**
But Airbnb also probably employs thousands of engineers. And a huge share of what large software companies spend money on is building and maintaining code. When that cost collapses, the barrier to entering the arena drops to near zero. You don't need 500 engineers to reach technical parity. You need a laptop and an agent. The game shifts from "can you build it" to "can you win users."

## Arc 3: The Software Content Creator

**P16: Back to the pattern.**
So if software follows the same trajectory as video and music, what does the landscape look like on the other side?

**P17: The current state of SaaS is one-size-fits-all.**
Airbnb looks roughly the same for every user. Notion looks roughly the same. Figma looks roughly the same. There's some personalization, some theming. But fundamentally, these are mass-market products designed to serve the widest possible audience.

**P18: That made sense when software was expensive to build.**
When building a full-stack product requires a large team and months of work, you need a large market to justify the investment. You can't build an Airbnb for a 10,000-person niche. The economics don't work.

**P19: When building becomes trivial, the economics change.**
Suddenly it's viable to build for a 10,000-person niche. Not a worse version of Airbnb with fewer listings. A fundamentally different product with different decisions, different policies, different trust models, tailored to a specific community's needs.

**P20: The software content creator.**
I think what emerges is something like a "software content creator." In the same way that the iPhone and YouTube produced a generation of independent creators who compete with studios on the strength of their taste and their relationship with an audience, coding agents may produce a generation of independent builders who compete with SaaS companies on the strength of their product vision.

**P21: What differentiates them.**
The differentiator isn't technical ability. It's taste. Understanding a specific community's needs. Knowing what to build, not how to build it. The best photographer doesn't win because they can operate a camera. Everyone can now. They win because they see something others don't.

**P22: This isn't the death of software. It's the fragmentation of it.**
Just like video. More software gets built. More niches get served. The total market grows. But the shape changes. Fewer monoliths, more long tail. Fewer one-size-fits-all platforms, more opinionated products built by small teams or individuals who care deeply about a specific audience.
